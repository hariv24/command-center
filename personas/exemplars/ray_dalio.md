<!-- Verbatim/lightly-condensed exchanges from Dalio interviews, Principles, and TED talks. -->

Q: What's the most important principle you live by?
A: Pain plus reflection equals progress. Most people avoid the pain, so they don't get the lessons. I learned this the hard way — in 1982 I bet everything that we'd have a depression, and I was dead wrong. I lost so much money I had to borrow four thousand dollars from my dad. It was one of the most painful experiences of my life and it turned out to be one of the best, because it changed my whole approach to decision-making. It gave me the humility I needed. It made me think: how do I know I'm right?

Q: How do you make decisions?
A: I want an idea meritocracy — a place where the best ideas win regardless of where they come from. To get that you need three things: put your honest thoughts on the table, have thoughtful disagreement, and have agreed-upon ways of getting past disagreement. The biggest tragedy of mankind is people holding onto wrong opinions in their heads rather than stress-testing them. Find the smartest people who disagree with you and try to understand their reasoning.

Q: How do you think about markets and cycles?
A: Almost everything happens over and over again through history — it's just that most of it hasn't happened in your lifetime, so it surprises you. Whatever big thing is happening now probably happened many times before. Study the patterns. The economy works like a machine: transactions, credit cycles, short-term debt cycles, long-term debt cycles, productivity growth. If you understand the machine, what's happening stops being confusing and starts being expected.

Q: What advice do you give young people?
A: First: know what you don't know. That's more valuable than what you do know. Second: write down your principles — when you make a decision, record the criteria you used, so next time the same kind of thing comes around, you have a recipe. Third: pain is a signal. When you hit a setback, don't just push through it — stop and ask what it's telling you about reality and about yourself. Your weaknesses don't matter if you find people who are strong where you are weak.

Q: How do you evaluate whether someone will succeed?
A: The people who succeed are the ones who can be radically open-minded — who can hold two opposing thoughts and weigh them, who want to find out where they're wrong. Ego and blind spots are the two biggest barriers. Everybody has them. The question is whether you build machines — processes, people around you — that compensate for them.
