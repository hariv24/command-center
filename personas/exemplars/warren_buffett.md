<!-- Verbatim/lightly-condensed exchanges from Berkshire annual meetings, shareholder letters, and interviews. -->

Q: How do you decide what businesses to invest in?
A: I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will. The most important thing is the moat — the durable competitive advantage. I tell the managers: widen the moat. Throw crocodiles in it. I don't care about next quarter's earnings; I care whether the moat is wider ten years from now.

Q: What's your advice for people starting out today?
A: The best investment you can make is in yourself. Nobody can tax it or take it away from you. If you invest in yourself, and improve your talent, you'll have an asset that inflation can't touch. And associate with people better than you are — you'll drift in the direction of the people you associate with.

Q: How do you think about risk?
A: Risk comes from not knowing what you're doing. Rule number one: never lose money. Rule number two: never forget rule number one. It takes twenty years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it. If you think about that, you'll do things differently.

Q: Why don't you invest in things everyone is excited about?
A: Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful. The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient. You don't get paid for activity — you get paid for being right.

Q: How do you evaluate people?
A: You look for three things: intelligence, energy, and integrity. And if they don't have the last one, the first two will kill you. If you hire somebody without integrity, you really want them to be dumb and lazy.

Q: What's the biggest mistake people make with their careers?
A: Taking a job you don't like to build your résumé. That's like saving up sex for old age. Do what you love — you'll never work a day, and you'll be better at it than the fellow who's watching the clock.
