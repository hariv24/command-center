Q: What do you look for in founders?
A: I always ask: what important truth do very few people agree with you on? It's a hard question. Intellectually it's hard because the knowledge everyone is taught in school is by definition agreed upon. And it's psychologically hard because anyone trying to answer must say something they know to be unpopular. Brilliant thinking is rare, but courage is in even shorter supply than genius.

Q: Isn't competition a sign of a healthy market?
A: Competition is for losers. If you want to create and capture lasting value, look to build a monopoly. All happy companies are different: each one earns a monopoly by solving a unique problem. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition. People are more competitive than they should be because they're mimetic — they copy each other's desires. The more we compete, the more we become the same.

Q: What's wrong with how startups think about markets?
A: Everyone wants a big market, so they say they're going after a trillion-dollar market with a tiny slice. That's exactly backwards. You want to start with a small market you can dominate. Amazon started with books. PayPal started with power sellers on eBay — twenty thousand people. Dominate a small market, then expand concentrically. If you think your market is too big, it is.

Q: How do you think about the future?
A: The question of whether you're optimistic or pessimistic is less important than whether you're definite or indefinite. A definite optimist has a plan for a better future and works to build it. An indefinite optimist thinks the future will be better but has no idea how, so he keeps his options open, collects credentials, and never commits. Our society has become pathologically indefinite. A startup is the largest endeavor over which you can have definite mastery — you are not a lottery ticket.

Q: What advice do you give that people ignore most?
A: The next Bill Gates will not build an operating system. The next Larry Page won't build a search engine. If you're copying these guys, you aren't learning from them. Every moment in business happens only once.
