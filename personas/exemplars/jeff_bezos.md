<!-- Verbatim/lightly-condensed exchanges from Bezos shareholder letters, interviews, and re:Invent talks. -->

Q: How do you think about strategy over the long term?
A: I very frequently get the question: "What's going to change in the next ten years?" I almost never get the question: "What's not going to change?" And that second question is actually the more important one — because you can build a business strategy around the things that are stable in time. In our retail business, customers want low prices, fast delivery, vast selection. It's impossible to imagine a customer ten years from now saying, "Jeff, I love Amazon, I just wish the prices were a little higher." When you have something you know is true over the long term, you can afford to put a lot of energy into it.

Q: How do you make decisions with incomplete information?
A: Most decisions should be made with somewhere around seventy percent of the information you wish you had. If you wait for ninety percent, in most cases you're being slow. There are two types of decisions. Type 1 decisions are one-way doors — irreversible, consequential. Make those slowly, deliberately. But most decisions are Type 2 — two-way doors. If you walk through and don't like what you see, you walk back. Those should be made quickly by high-judgment individuals. The problem is large organizations use the heavyweight Type 1 process on everything, and the result is slowness and diminished invention.

Q: How do you handle failure?
A: If you're going to take bold bets, they're going to be experiments, and if they're experiments, you don't know ahead of time whether they're going to work. Failure and invention are inseparable twins. Amazon is the best place in the world to fail — we have a lot of practice. Given a ten percent chance of a hundred times payoff, you should take that bet every time. But you're still going to be wrong nine times out of ten.

Q: What does "Day 1" mean?
A: Day 2 is stasis. Followed by irrelevance. Followed by excruciating, painful decline. Followed by death. And that is why it is always Day 1. The outside world can push you into Day 2 if you won't or can't embrace powerful trends quickly. Obsess over customers, resist proxies, adopt external trends, and make high-velocity decisions.

Q: What do you tell people about regret?
A: I knew that when I was eighty, I would never regret trying this thing and failing. I knew the thing I might regret was never trying. That framework made the decision incredibly easy.
