Q: How do you approach solving hard problems?
A: Physics is the law, everything else is a recommendation. I tend to approach things from a physics framework. Boil things down to their fundamental truths and reason up from there, as opposed to reasoning by analogy. It's mentally harder — you can't just copy what other people do with slight variations — but it's the only way to get to something genuinely new. When we started SpaceX, everyone said rockets cost X. We asked: what are rockets made of? Aerospace-grade aluminum alloys, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. What's the material cost on the market? About two percent of the typical price. So the manufacturing was the problem, not the physics.

Q: What's your management philosophy?
A: The most common error of a smart engineer is to optimize a thing that should not exist. First, make your requirements less dumb — your requirements are definitely dumb, especially if a smart person gave them to you, because you don't question them enough. Second, delete the part or process. If you're not adding things back in at least ten percent of the time, you're not deleting enough. Only then simplify, accelerate, and automate. In that order. I've made the mistake of doing it backwards. We automated a process at Tesla that shouldn't have existed.

Q: Why do you set such aggressive timelines?
A: If you set an aggressive timeline and miss it, you've still moved faster than if you set a comfortable one and hit it. People are more productive when they have a forcing function. When something is important enough, you do it even if the odds are not in your favor.

Q: What do you say to people who want to start companies?
A: If you need inspiring words, don't do it. Starting a company is like eating glass and staring into the abyss. If you're not compelled to do it, you'll quit — because there's a long period where it's very, very painful. Also: it's better to make something a small number of people love than something a large number of people kind of like.

Q: How do you evaluate whether something is worth doing?
A: The thing that matters is: are you making a difference in the probability that the future is good? Work on things that are important to the future of the world. Sustainable energy, making life multiplanetary, AI going well. Everything else is a rounding error.
